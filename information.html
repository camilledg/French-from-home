Below is a example of what a Beginner lesson could be like. If you like what you see, you can register by filling out the form on the Register page./em>

The lessons could look like something along these lines: how to introduce yourself, say what you like, what you dislike; or introduce prepositions and numbers into your vocabulary, how to tell the time and the basic tenses (present, passe compose, future proche). Basically, we’ll reviews basic grammar and vocabulary points and integrate them into our chats. I could, for example, tell you what future proche is, what it’s for, how and when to use it, and we’ll practise. I could ask you to tell me what you intend to do after the lesson is over, or what you’ll eat for supper. All of it using simple, casual words. We can speak in English or in French - we’ll go with what you feel like doing.

Below is a example of what an Intermediate lesson could be like. If you like what you see, you can register by filling out the form on the Register page./em>

Here, we could cover some trickier or general aspects of French. Again, this serves as an example only. We’ll adjust the format and the content together. For example, we could work on more complicated tense like imparfait or passé-composé, etc, whilst working toward developing a natural, conversational French. We could cultivate enough vocabulary to start expressing opinions with confidence, and even understand jokes. The goal here is to practise the basics as much as possible whilst broadening your understanding of the French culture.

This last category might be the easiest, to some extent. With Advanced, all we would do is talk. The topics could range from European politics to baking techniques, from classic rock to France’s history - you tell me. Or we could talk about your week, your hobbies, what you’ve been up to during lockdown if you’d like. I could send you a list of additional vocabulary if you want it. This is all up to you, it’s a conversation, pure and simple.

Even as schools and nurseries are now closed in the UK, children shouldn't stop practicing French.

When you're young, picking up a language is easy. But hour-long lessons focusing on the grammatical side of things aren't. I'm offering much smaller sessions, twice a week, focusing just on speaking French. I divide the sessions between age groups (from 5 to 8 years-old, a 15-minutes session; from 9 to 12 years-old, a 30-minutes session). No strict curriculum involved - I'm here to read stories, practise pronounciation, and play games!
